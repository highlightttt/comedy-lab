💡 Concept Collision

Dating Apps = Second Immigration Interview

The thesis: filling out a dating profile as an immigrant is just another visa application. You're selling yourself to a country that already let you in but hasn't fully accepted you. Swipe right = approved. Swipe left = denied without explanation — just like the embassy.

🎯 PUNCH / 💡 THESIS

Redefining "Match"

Match = someone who tolerates your origin story. Unmatch = deportation from someone's love life.

  1. Dating apps are just immigration interviews with better lighting. "Where are you from?" "What do you do?" "How long are you planning to stay?"🔥
  2. My dating profile is basically my visa application with a selfie. Same energy. "I promise I will contribute to this relationship and not overstay."
  3. Getting unmatched is just emotional deportation. No explanation. No appeal process. One day you're in, next day — gone.🔥
  4. Americans put "I love to travel" on their profile. Immigrants put "I already traveled. I'm here. That was the hard part."
  5. "What are you looking for?" Long-term. I'm always looking for long-term. My visa, my lease, my relationship — I need stability in at least one of them.🔥
🧱 BUILD

The Bio Problem

Writing a bio in your second language = being charming with a handicap.

  1. Writing a witty bio in your second language is like boxing with one hand. You know the joke. You just can't land it.
  2. In Chinese I'm funny. In English I'm "interesting." That's the word people use when they can't figure out if you're smart or just confusing.🔥
  3. My friend said "just be yourself on the app." Bro, which self? The Chinese self who's polite and reserved, or the American self who pretends to like hiking?
  4. Every American dating profile: "I love adventures." My biggest adventure was getting through customs. You want adventure? Try explaining your job title in broken English to a TSA agent.
  5. Dating app said "add your height." I'm 5'8". In China that's above average. In America that's a swipe left. Immigration shrunk me two inches.

💡 Concept Collision

AI Is Taking Your Job? Immigrants Already Did That.

The thesis: Americans are scared of AI the way they used to be scared of immigrants. "They're taking our jobs" — we've heard this before. Immigrants are the original AI: cheap, efficient, doesn't complain, works 24/7.

🎯 PUNCH / 💡 THESIS

Immigrant = Original AI

We were the automation before automation existed. AI just does what immigrants did but without the accent.

  1. Everyone's worried AI will take their jobs. Immigrants are like — "First time?"🔥
  2. "AI works faster, cheaper, never complains." That's literally my dad's resume. He's been AI since 1998.🔥
  3. The difference between AI and immigrants: AI hallucinates. Immigrants just exaggerate on their resume. Same thing, one gets funded.
  4. AI doesn't need a visa. AI doesn't need healthcare. AI doesn't send money home. Honestly? America finally found the perfect immigrant.🔥
  5. They trained AI on the entire internet and it still can't do what my mom does — guilt-trip you into eating more in three languages.
🧱 BUILD / 🔄 CALLBACK

AI Anxiety × Immigrant Experience

The real collision: Americans now feel what immigrants always felt — replaceable, disposable, competing against something that works harder for less.

  1. "AI will make us all unemployed." Welcome to the immigrant experience. We've been feeling replaceable since day one. At least you get a severance package.
  2. My company replaced the intern with ChatGPT. The intern was me five years ago. I've been replaced by my replacement's replacement.
  3. AI can write code, write essays, write emails. But it still can't write a WeChat message to your Chinese boss that's exactly the right amount of respectful without being too respectful. That's a skill.
  4. If AI takes all the tech jobs, immigrants will just pivot. We've been pivoting since we got here. First restaurant, then nail salon, then tech, now what — AI repair?
  5. AI is basically an immigrant who made it. Came from nothing, learned everything online, now does your job better. But nobody's asking AI "where are you REALLY from?"🔥

💡 Word Redefinition

Bilingual = Two Different People Sharing One Body

The thesis: speaking two languages isn't a skill — it's a personality disorder. Chinese-you and English-you are different people. They have different opinions, different humor, different confidence levels. Immigration didn't move you to a new country. It split you into two people.

🎯 PUNCH / 💡 THESIS

The Two Yous

Chinese me is assertive, quick, funny. English me is "sorry, can you repeat that?" They're not the same person.

  1. Being bilingual isn't a skill, it's a personality disorder. Chinese me is confident and decisive. English me says "sorry" before every sentence.🔥
  2. In Chinese I argue with my mom for 45 minutes. In English I can't even argue with a barista about my wrong order. "Actually this is fine."
  3. My IQ drops 30 points when I switch to English. I'm a philosopher in Chinese and a caveman in English. "Me want... the thing... for the food... you know?"
  4. I dream in Chinese but have nightmares in English. Specifically, the nightmare where someone asks "can you say something in Chinese?" at a party.
  5. English me is polite. Chinese me is honest. They've never met. If they did, English me would apologize and Chinese me would say "why are you apologizing?"🔥
🧱 BUILD / 🎤 CROWDWORK

Lost in Translation (Literally)

Some concepts don't survive the border crossing. "Add oil" is encouragement in Chinese and a car maintenance tip in English.

  1. Some Chinese concepts just don't translate. "加油" means "you can do it!" Literally it means "add oil." I tried saying "add oil!" to my American coworker during a deadline. He thought I was having a stroke.
  2. Americans say "it is what it is." Chinese people say "没办法" — literally "there's no method." Same resignation, but the Chinese version sounds like you actually tried to find a solution first.
  3. The hardest English word for Chinese people isn't "rural" or "squirrel." It's "no." We physically cannot say no. We say "maybe next time" which means no. "I'll think about it" which means no. "Sounds interesting" which means absolutely not.🔥
  4. In English, "How are you?" means nothing. In Chinese, if someone asks how you are, they actually want to know. And then give you advice. And then tell your parents.
  5. 🎤 Crowd work seed: "Anyone here bilingual? What's a word in your language that just CANNOT be translated? ... See, every immigrant has one. That untranslatable word is basically your cultural identity compressed into a sound."
